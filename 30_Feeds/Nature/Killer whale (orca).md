Orcas (killer whales) are actually the largest dolphins, and they’re peculiar in the dolphin family because different populations have their own cultures: distinct hunting strategies, dialects, and social traditions that calves must learn from elders rather than being born with them. These cultural differences explain why some orca groups hunt fish, others sharks or whales, others seals on beaches, and even why some have started ramming boat rudders.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

***

## 1. Orcas within the dolphin family

- Taxonomy: Orcas (*Orcinus orca*) are toothed whales in the oceanic dolphin superfamily Delphinidae, making them dolphins rather than “true” large whales.[^2][^5]
- Size: They are the largest dolphin species, reaching around 9 m in length and up to about 6 tonnes, so big that they’re similar in size to some baleen whales.[^1]
- Global distribution: Orcas live in all oceans, from polar seas to tropical waters, and occupy the apex-predator niche, feeding on fish, squid, marine mammals, and even other dolphins and whales.[^5][^2][^1]

What makes them unusual among dolphins is the combination of extreme ecological flexibility (many prey types) and strong cultural specialization (each population typically focuses on a narrow prey set and learned behaviors).[^3][^4][^2]

***

## 2. Social structure and “culture”

- Pods and matrilines: Orcas typically live in tight-knit matrilineal pods, where both males and females stay with their mothers for life in many populations.[^2][^3]
- Matriarchs: Older females lead groups, share food, navigate, and serve as the main “teachers” of hunting and movement patterns.[^3][^2]
- Longevity: They can live 60 years or more, giving plenty of time to accumulate experience and pass it down through generations.[^3]

Researchers describe orcas as having culture: suites of behaviors (diet choices, hunting styles, social rituals, play) that are learned socially and differ between groups that live in the same region but don’t interbreed or share habits.[^6][^2][^3]

***

## 3. Ecotypes: different “kinds” of orcas

Even within the same ocean, orcas split into ecotypes that look similar but live very different lifestyles.[^6][^3]

- Northeast Pacific “resident” orcas
    - Diet: Mainly fish, especially chinook and chum salmon.[^3]
    - Social life: Very tight family pods; highly vocal, calling loudly even while hunting.[^2][^3]
- Bigg’s (transient) orcas
    - Diet: Marine mammals such as seals, porpoises, and sea lions.[^6][^3]
    - Behavior: Travel in smaller groups, roam more widely, and hunt silently to avoid alerting their mammalian prey.[^7][^2][^3]
- Offshore orcas
    - Diet: Eat so many sharks that their teeth are worn down from the rough skin; an offshore “flat-toothed” ecotype around South Africa and New Zealand specializes in sharks and other marine mammals.[^4][^3]
- Antarctic ecotypes
    - Some specialize in penguins; others in minke whales; “pack ice” orcas focus on seals on ice floes.[^4][^3]

These ecotypes coexist but have different dialects, hunting methods, and food preferences, and they rarely interbreed, showing how strongly culture and ecology partition the species.[^7][^6][^3]

***

## 4. Communication, dialects, and teaching

- Sound types: Orcas use pulsed calls, clicks, and whistles; clicks are used for echolocation and also communication.[^2]
- Dialects: Each pod has a distinct call repertoire, and groups within the same region can have dialect differences analogous to human accents; resident clans in the Pacific Northwest have dialects as different as “Greek vs Russian,” with pods having local “Southern drawl vs New England accent” variants.[^3]
- Vocal learning: Calves selectively learn calls from their mothers, even when other killer whales are present, and end up with a call set matching their matriline.[^7]

Teaching is active, not passive. Matriarchs lead hunts, adjust behavior, and even deliberately put calves into training scenarios (for example gradually exposing them to being partly beached) so they can practice difficult techniques safely.[^8][^4][^2]

***

## 5. Major hunting strategies (overview)

Across the world, orcas show a toolbox of specialized strategies, with each pod typically mastering a subset that fits its environment and prey.[^4][^2][^3]

Key strategies include:

- Carousel feeding on schooling fish (e.g., herring).
- Wave-washing seals off ice floes.
- Intentional stranding (beaching) to grab seals or sea lions on shore.
- Shark and ray hunting using tonic immobilization and liver targeting.
- Cooperative attacks on dolphins, whales, and other mammals.
- Experimental or play-like behaviors that can spread socially, such as boat-rudder ramming.

Each technique is learned socially, refined over generations, and often strongly associated with a single region or clan.[^8][^4][^2]

***

## 6. Carousel feeding on fish

In Norwegian and North Atlantic waters, orcas that specialize on herring use highly coordinated “carousel” hunts.[^9][^4]

- Herding: Orcas drive herring into dense bait balls within the upper tens of meters of the water column, sometimes using calls in the fishes’ most sensitive hearing range to scatter and bunch them.[^9][^4]
- Visual control: They flash their white bellies around the bait ball to keep the fish tightly packed.[^4]
- Tail-slapping: Individuals swim through or near the ball and suddenly U‑turn, slamming their powerful tail flukes into the fish to stun or kill them.[^9][^4]
- Picking off prey: The orcas then calmly eat stunned fish one by one, often sharing with relatives.[^4][^3]

Studies show these hunts involve role division and geometrical patterns of movement between pairs, suggesting a learned, optimized cooperative strategy, not just opportunistic feeding.[^9]

***

## 7. Wave-washing seals off ice

In the Antarctic, “pack ice” orcas have developed a wave-washing technique to hunt seals resting on floes.[^2][^4]

- Scouting: Orcas spyhop (rise vertically to look above water) to locate seals on ice sheets.[^2][^4]
- Planning: A matriarch appears to coordinate the attack, choosing the angle and number of whales needed to generate the right wave.[^4]
- Wave creation: Several orcas line up and swim in formation, then dive under the ice floe, producing a wave that breaks the floe into smaller pieces and pushes them into open water.[^2][^4]
- Final wash: A subsequent wave knocks the seal off its shrinking floe into the water, where the orcas have the advantage.[^4][^2]

This technique requires precise timing and group coordination; juveniles watch from the side and, over time, attempt smaller roles before becoming full participants.[^2][^4]

***

## 8. Intentional stranding (beaching) to hunt

Some of the most dramatic orca behavior occurs off Peninsula Valdés in Argentina and around the Crozet Islands in the southern Indian Ocean.[^8][^4][^2]

- Behavior: Orcas deliberately ride waves onto gravel beaches to grab seal or sea lion pups, temporarily stranding themselves on land.[^8][^4]
- Risk: Being stranded is dangerous; whales can die if they can’t slide back into the water, so this behavior goes against a deep survival instinct.[^8][^2]
- Learning: Observations since the 1970s show the same pods performing this technique hundreds of times, passing it down like an “intellectual heirloom” from older whales to calves.[^8]

Teaching process:

- Matriarchs gradually bring calves closer to the shore, at first allowing only partial stranding while blocking their escape so they get used to being partly out of water.[^8][^2]
- Over time, the distance and time beached are increased until juveniles are comfortable performing full beaching attacks.[^8][^2]
- Not all whales master the technique; some remain observers, illustrating that individual learning and aptitude matter.[^4][^8]

***

## 9. Shark and ray hunting, including great whites

In some regions, especially off South Africa and in offshore ecotypes, orcas focus on sharks and rays.[^2][^4]

- “Flat-toothed” ecotype: An unofficial offshore type in South Africa and near New Zealand specializes in sharks and other mammals; tooth wear from abrasive shark skin is a key clue.[^3][^4]
- Tonic immobilization: A pair of males nicknamed Port and Starboard have been seen flipping great white sharks and other shark species upside down, inducing tonic immobilization (a temporary dazed state) before extracting only the liver, which is nutrient-rich.[^2]
- Liver targeting: Washed-up great white carcasses with missing livers along South Africa’s coast implicate these orcas and show a precise, repeated method.[^2]

Continuous hunting of sharks with similar tactics suggests social learning and a shared “recipe” for shark hunts within certain pods.[^4][^2]

***

## 10. Hunting dolphins, porpoises, and other whales

Mammal-eating orcas elsewhere use high-speed, high-impact, and deceptive strategies.[^4][^2]

- Dolphin hunts: Eastern tropical Pacific orcas sometimes launch themselves more than 6 m out of the water, striking dolphins with their bodies to stun or exhaust them, while the pod coordinates to intercept escape routes.[^4]
- Mimicry against humpbacks: Mammal-eating orcas may steal humpback calves by waiting until the mother is vulnerable, then kidnapping the calf and carrying it on their back in the slipstream, mimicking how the mother would transport it.[^4]
- Silence: To avoid detection, mammal-hunting orcas often switch off their usual loud calls and stay acoustically quiet during the final approach.[^2][^4]

Mammal hunts show elaborate planning and patience, with orcas waiting for the optimal moment and using cooperative chases, ambushes, and deception.[^2][^4]

***

## 11. Cooperation with seabirds and “target practice”

Orcas sometimes integrate other species into their foraging strategy, particularly seabirds.[^10][^11]

- Using birds as indicators: In places like Norway and the North Pacific, orcas watch for diving seabirds, using them as “radar” to locate dense fish schools more efficiently.[^11]
- Learning: Young orcas learn to read these cues by following their mothers and experienced pod members, who steer toward areas where birds are actively feeding.[^11]
- Target practice: Some accounts describe orcas using birds, porpoises, or other wildlife as “target practice,” honing attack precision and coordination beyond immediate feeding needs.[^11][^4]

This kind of learning emphasizes flexibility: orcas can incorporate the behavior of other animals into their own hunting routines.[^10][^11]

***

## 12. Non-feeding behaviors and play

Orcas show many behaviors that are not directly about getting food but are culturally variable and often playful.[^3][^2]

- Rubbing beaches: Some resident pods in British Columbia regularly visit specific “rubbing beaches” where they scrape their bodies along smooth pebbles, while neighboring pods ignore these sites entirely, indicating a learned local tradition.[^3]
- Greeting ceremonies: In the Salish Sea, residents sometimes line up in two facing rows and then mix in a burst of physical contact and vocalizations, like a social “mosh pit,” when pods meet.[^3]
- Play: Orcas leap, tail‑slap, and spyhop in patterns that differ between groups; these displays may reinforce social bonds or practice motor skills.[^3][^2]

At captive facilities and in the wild, orcas have invented novel game-like behaviors, such as using fish pieces to lure seabirds close enough to catch them, showing creativity and innovation.[^10][^2]

***

## 13. Boat-rudder attacks and social spread

Since around 2020, a group of orcas in the Strait of Gibraltar region, known as the “Gladis” group, has repeatedly interacted with and sometimes damaged sailboat rudders.[^12][^2]

- Behavior: At least 15 orcas have been observed ramming boats, focusing on the rudder, and often disabling steering; over 700 interactions have been recorded.[^2]
- Hypotheses: One theory is that a female dubbed White Gladis was injured by a boat, leading to “revenge” attacks; another is that this started as a novel play behavior that spread through the pod by social learning.[^12][^2]
- Teaching: Observers have seen White Gladis leading her offspring into ramming boats, suggesting active demonstration of the behavior to younger whales.[^2]

Whether motivated by trauma, curiosity, or play, the pattern’s spread within the Gladis group mirrors how new hunting strategies and traditions propagate in orca culture.[^3][^2]

***

## 14. How and where orcas learn

Across all these examples, the learning pattern is consistent and very different from most other dolphins.[^8][^3][^2]

Key elements:

- High parental investment: Mothers and other adults invest significant time in teaching calves specific hunting techniques, including risky ones like beaching.[^8][^2]
- Apprenticeship: Calves first observe, then practice under supervision, often failing many times before succeeding; for beach hunts, success rates are under half, yet food is shared with the whole family so learners are still fed.[^8][^4]
- Social transmission: Behaviors such as wave-washing, intentional stranding, shark liver extraction, rubbing-beach visits, and possibly boat attacks are confined to particular pods or regions and persist across decades, indicating they are socially transmitted traditions.[^8][^3][^2]
- Dialect-based identity: Since calves learn their mother’s calls, vocal dialects mark cultural membership and help maintain boundaries between fish‑eating and mammal‑eating groups that share the same waters but do not mix.[^7][^6][^3]

In other words, an orca’s behavior is shaped not just by genes or environment, but by which “family” it is born into and what that family chooses to teach.[^3][^2]

***

## 15. Peculiarities compared with other dolphins

Relative to other dolphins, orcas stand out in several ways:[^5][^1][^3][^2]

- Scale of specialization: Many dolphins have preferred prey, but orca ecotypes show extreme divergence in diet, from salmon specialists to shark-eaters, often in the same region.[^3][^4]
- Cultural depth: They have stable matrilines, strong dialects, and highly specific traditions (rubbing beaches, greeting ceremonies, beach hunting) that don’t appear in neighboring orca populations or other dolphin species.[^7][^3]
- Cognitive flexibility: Orcas display creativity and innovation in both wild and managed settings, inventing new behaviors (like using bait to catch birds) and adapting to human-made environments (e.g., boats) in complex ways.[^10][^2]
- Human interactions: Despite being apex predators capable of killing large whales, no confirmed cases of wild orcas killing humans are known, which contrasts sharply with their “killer whale” reputation.[^13][^2]

Taken together, this makes orcas a powerful case study in non-human culture: a single dolphin species whose members can live like very different “peoples,” each with its own language, diet, hunting tactics, and traditions—all learned and maintained socially.[^4][^3][^2]
