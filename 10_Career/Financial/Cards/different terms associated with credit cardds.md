# Notes on Credit Cards

## Overview
Credit cards are borrowed-money payment tools that let you spend now and pay later, while debit cards spend money directly from your bank account. The main things to understand are billing cycle, statement date, due date, minimum due, interest, fees, and rewards.

## Core Terms
- **Credit limit**: The maximum amount the bank lets you borrow on the card.
- **Available credit**: The remaining amount you can still spend before reaching your limit.
- **Outstanding balance**: The total amount you currently owe on the card.
- **Statement balance**: The amount that appears on your monthly bill for a billing cycle.
- **Transaction date**: The day you made the purchase.
- **Posting date**: The day the transaction is recorded by the bank.
- **Statement date**: The day the bank closes the billing cycle and generates the bill.
- **Due date**: The last date to make payment for that bill.
- **Minimum due**: The smallest amount you can pay to avoid late fees and keep the card active.
- **APR / interest rate**: The yearly cost of borrowing if you do not pay in full.
- **Grace period / interest-free period**: The time when purchases do not earn interest, usually only if you pay the full statement balance on time.
- **Cash advance**: Withdrawing cash using the credit card.
- **EMI**: Converting a purchase into fixed monthly installments.
- **Reward points / cashback / miles**: Benefits you earn for spending on the card.
- **Forex / foreign transaction fee**: Extra charge for spending in another currency.
- **Merchant discount rate (MDR)**: The fee merchants pay when customers use cards.

## How a Credit Card Works
When you use a credit card, the bank pays the merchant first and records that amount as your debt. At the end of the billing cycle, the bank sends a statement showing what you spent, what you owe, the minimum due, and the due date. If you pay the full statement balance by the due date, you usually avoid interest on normal purchases.

## Billing Cycle
The **billing cycle** is the period during which your card transactions are collected before the bill is made. For example, if your statement date is the 5th of each month, the billing cycle may run from the 6th of the previous month to the 5th of the current month. Purchases made after the statement date go into the next bill.

## Statement Date and Due Date
The **statement date** is when the cycle closes and the bank creates the bill. The **due date** is the deadline to pay that bill. A card often gives you a gap of a few days to a few weeks between these two dates, and this is part of the interest-free period for purchases if you pay in full.

## Minimum Due
The **minimum due** is the smallest payment the bank asks for by the due date. It is usually a small percentage of your total balance plus any fees, EMIs, or past unpaid amounts. Paying only the minimum due avoids a late fee, but interest continues on the remaining balance, so the debt stays active.

## Interest and Interest-Free Period
The **interest-free period** applies mainly to regular purchases and usually works only if you pay the full statement balance on time. If you pay only part of the bill, the unpaid amount may start accruing interest, and in many card terms the new purchases may also lose the interest-free benefit until the balance is cleared. **Cash advances** are different because interest often starts immediately.

## Common Credit Card Fees
- **Joining fee**: One-time fee when you get the card.
- **Annual fee**: Yearly fee for keeping the card.
- **Renewal fee**: Another name for annual fee on some cards.
- **Late payment fee**: Charged if you pay after the due date.
- **Over-limit fee**: Charged if you spend beyond your credit limit.
- **Cash advance fee**: Fee for withdrawing cash from the card.
- **Interest / finance charge**: Cost charged on unpaid balances.
- **Balance transfer fee**: Fee for moving debt from one card to another.
- **Foreign transaction fee**: Extra fee on international or foreign-currency spending.
- **EMI processing fee**: Fee for converting a purchase into installments.
- **Card replacement fee**: Charged if you lose the card or need a reissue.
- **Cheque bounce / payment return fee**: Charged if a payment method fails.
- **GST on charges**: Tax added to fees, interest, and some card services.
- **Fuel surcharge / railway surcharge**: Extra fee on some transport-related payments.

## How Banks Profit
Banks earn from credit cards in several ways. They make money from interest when customers carry balances, from fees like annual or late payment charges, and from merchant fees paid every time a card is used at a store. Even if a customer always pays in full, the bank can still earn from merchant processing fees.

## Debit Card vs Credit Card
- **Debit card**: Uses your own bank balance immediately; usually no borrowing, no interest, and low debt risk.
- **Credit card**: Uses borrowed money; offers an interest-free period, rewards, and credit-building benefits, but can become expensive if unpaid.
- **Debit card spending** is usually simpler and safer for budgeting.
- **Credit card spending** is more flexible and can give more perks, but it requires discipline.

## Rewards
Rewards exist because banks want people to use the card more often. Cashback, points, and miles encourage spending, build loyalty, and increase the fee income banks earn from transactions. The reward provider benefits because higher usage usually means more revenue, more customer retention, and more future spending.

## Why Banks Promote Cards
Banks market credit cards aggressively using welcome bonuses, reward offers, premium status, and limited-time deals. These offers make the card feel valuable and can persuade people to apply and spend more. The bank’s goal is to increase transaction volume, fee income, and interest income from unpaid balances.

## Risks for Users
The biggest risk is spending more than you can repay. If you pay only the minimum due repeatedly, the remaining balance can grow because of interest and fees. This can reduce your credit score, create debt stress, and make future borrowing harder.

## Simple Example
If your card bill is ₹10,000 and the minimum due is ₹500, paying only ₹500 avoids a late fee but leaves ₹9,500 unpaid. That unpaid amount can keep attracting interest. If you pay the full ₹10,000 by the due date, you usually avoid interest on normal purchases.

## Key Takeaway
Credit cards are useful tools when used like a short-term payment method and paid off fully each month. They become expensive when treated like long-term borrowing. The most important terms to remember are billing cycle, statement date, due date, minimum due, and interest-free period.
