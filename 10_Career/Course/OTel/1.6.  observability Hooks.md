# Course Notes: LFS148 Getting Started with OpenTelemetry
**Section:** [Why Do We Need OpenTelemetry? / Problems with the Status Quo](https://trainingportal.linuxfoundation.org/learn/course/getting-started-with-opentelemetry-lfs148/why-do-we-need-opentelemetry/problems-with-the-status-quo?page=4)
**Topic:** No Built-in Instrumentation in Open Source Software

---

## 1. The Core Telemetry Gap in Open Source
* **The Reality of Modern Apps:** Most modern applications are heavily reliant on third-party open-source libraries, frameworks, and standalone components.
* **Where Execution Happens:** Because the majority of code execution happens *outside* of the developer's custom business logic (within these open-source dependencies), collecting telemetry from these components is absolutely critical.
* **The Knowledge Paradox:** The maintainers and developers of open-source software have the deepest understanding of what metrics, logs, or events are important to track. However, they currently lack a standardized, native way to build this telemetry directly into their projects.

---

## 2. Flawed Alternative: Choosing a Specific Observability Vendor
One approach is for maintainers to choose a specific observability vendor's instrumentation toolset, but this introduces major trade-offs:
* **Dependencies & Constraints:** It forces unnecessary third-party dependencies onto the open-source project and forces downstream users to integrate that specific vendor's ecosystem into their own infrastructure.
* **The Tracing Impossible Barrier:** * While running multiple disparate logging and metrics tools is highly impractical, it is technically possible.
    * In contrast, **distributed tracing is completely impossible** without a shared standard, because every system component must agree on a unified format for trace context propagation to function correctly.

---

## 3. Flawed Alternative: Observability Hooks (Layer of Indirection)
To avoid vendor lock-in, open-source maintainers frequently resort to adding a layer of indirection by exposing generic "observability hooks." This shifts the burden onto the user, who must write custom adapters to pipe these hooks into their preferred monitoring backend.

### Associated Problems with Hooks:
* **High Maintenance Overhead:** Every time the open-source library updates, users must manually check, update, and re-test their custom adapters to ensure they don't break.
* **Performance Penalties:** The extra layer of indirection increases computational overhead because telemetry data must be constantly translated between different formats.
